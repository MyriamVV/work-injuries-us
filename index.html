As work-related injuries decreased during Covid-19, workers entered a coverage limbo

Myriam Vidal Valero

The Covid-19 pandemic forced people to transition to a remote work environment. This impacted in the number of work-related injuries, but also the way employers get compensated when they get injured.

DEC. 16, 2021

Covid-19 changed the way workers get injured. Photo: Foresight Commercial Insurance

After United States citizens went into lock down in 2020, the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) documented a decrease in work-related injuries from 2019.

Although this would seem like a positive outcome of the pandemic, it also became a source of concern about how to address work injuries. If the accident happened at home but during working hours, should it be covered? What about if there were no witnesses?

According to experts from Bricker & Eckler Attorneys at law, if the injury didn’t occur when the employee was doing a task for the employer, “the remote injury is less likely to be compensable.” But the debate keeps going.
